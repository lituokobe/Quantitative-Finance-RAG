# Understanding Rho: Definition, Uses, and Calculations for Options

## What Is Rho?

Rho measures how the price of an option changes in response to shifts in the risk-free interest rate. It reflects the sensitivity of calls and puts to rate changes; calls typically gain value as rates rise, while puts often lose value.

Though generally less influential than other "Greeks," like delta or theta, rho becomes more significant for options with long times to expiration. As part of the Greeks used in the Black-Scholes pricing model, rho helps traders and portfolio managers assess and manage interest-rate risk.

### Key Takeaways

- Rho measures how an option's price changes with a 1% change in interest rates.
- Options with longer time to expiration have a higher rho and are more sensitive to interest rate changes.
- Call options generally have positive rho, increasing in value when interest rates rise, while put options have negative rho.

## Understanding Rho Calculations and Practical Applications

Rho is calculated as the first derivative of an option's value concerning the risk-free rate. Rho predicts how an option's price changes with a 1% change in the risk-free rate of a U.S. Treasury bill.

For example, assume that a call option is priced at \$4 and has a rho of 0.25. If the risk-free rate rises 1 percent, say from 3 percent to 4 percent, the value of the call option would rise from \$4 to \$4.25.

Call options usually increase in price with rising interest rates, while put options decrease. Thus, call options have positive rho, while put options have negative rho.

Assume that[put option is priced at \$9 and has a rho of -0.35. If interest rates were to decrease from 5 percent to 4 percent, then the price of this put option would increase from \$9 to \$9.35. In this same scenario, assuming the call option mentioned above, its price would decrease from \$4 to \$3.75.

Rho is larger for in-the-money options and decreases as options become out-of-the-money. Also, rho increases as the time to expiration increases. Long-term equity anticipation securities (LEAPs), which are options that generally have expiration dates that are greater than one year away, are far more sensitive to changes in the risk-free rate and, thus, have larger rho than shorter-term options.

Although rho is key in the Black-Scholes model, interest rate changes only slightly affect option prices. Because of this, rho is usually considered to be the least important of all the option Greeks.

## The Bottom Line

Rho measures how an option's price responds to changes in the risk-free interest rate. Rising rates typically boost the value of call options while lowering the value of puts. Though rho is part of the Black-Scholes model, it's often viewed as the least influential of the Greeks.

Its impact becomes more pronounced for long-duration options such as LEAPs, which are more sensitive to rate shifts. Understanding rho can help investors anticipate these effects and adjust their portfolios to better manage interest-rate risk.
